History of Treaty 6

and its impact on today

Treaty 6, signed in 1876, is one of the key agreements between the Canadian government and Indigenous peoples, including the Cree, Dene, Stoney Dakota, Saulteaux, and Assiniboine nations, covering parts of present-day Alberta and Saskatchewan. The treaty promised Indigenous peoples annual payments, tools, provisions, and the establishment of reserves. It also assured them the right to hunt, fish, and trap on the land, even after settlers arrived. However, there have been disputes over the interpretation of the treaty, with Indigenous groups claiming they were misled and that the government did not fulfill its promises. The treaty continues to hold significant importance in discussions surrounding Indigenous rights, land, and reconciliation in Canada. Source: Government of Canada.

Treaty 6, signed in 1876, was a pivotal agreement between the Crown and several First Nations in what is now central Saskatchewan and Alberta. It was part of Canada's larger effort to secure land for settlement while promising Indigenous peoples protection and assistance. However, its history is marked by complex negotiations, differing interpretations, and long-term consequences.

Many Indigenous leaders, like Chief Mistawasis and Chief Ahtahkakoop, saw the treaty as a means of ensuring survival in the face of disease, declining buffalo populations, and increasing settler encroachment. They sought guarantees of aid, education, healthcare, and economic support. The treaty’s famous "medicine chest clause" was later interpreted by First Nations as a promise of universal healthcare, while the Crown viewed it as a limited provision. Source: Southern Chiefs Organization.

The Canadian government, led by officials like Alexander Morris, saw Treaty 6 as a way to secure land for settlement and resource extraction while preventing potential Indigenous resistance. The terms, however, were often not fully explained or were deliberately misleading. For instance, the concept of land "cession" was framed in ways that did not align with Indigenous understandings of land stewardship and shared use.

In the years following, many treaty promises were either delayed, ignored, or reinterpreted, contributing to poverty, loss of land, and government control over Indigenous lives through policies like the pass system, residential schools, and the Indian Act.

Today, Treaty 6 remains a key point of contention in Indigenous-Crown relations, with First Nations advocating for the fulfillment of promises and the recognition of their treaty rights as legally and morally binding. Source: Southern Chiefs Organization.

A cool fact about Treaty 6 is that during the negotiations in 1876, Cree leaders insisted on including a "famine and pestilence" clause because they foresaw the devastating impact of buffalo extinction—long before settlers fully realized it. This showed their deep environmental knowledge and foresight. The clause was meant to ensure government aid in times of crisis, but many of these promises were later broken, fueling long-standing disputes over treaty rights today. Source: University of Saskatchewan.
